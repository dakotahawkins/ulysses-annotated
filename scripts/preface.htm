Preface

For many of us, James Joyce’s Ulysses is the most richly satisfying novel ever written. Like Shakespeare’s plays or Homer’s epic poems it can be read again and again, each time with increased understanding and pleasure. It is one of those literary texts whose possibilities never come close to being exhausted.

But anyone hoping to tackle this monster for the first time is in for a struggle. Dozens of unfamiliar allusions teem on every page, commenting on the action in precise, subtle, and multifarious ways. Hundreds of musical references beg not only to be understood, but heard. Puzzles and enigmas lurk everywhere, asking readers to weigh evidence and track down leads like detectives. Past events seep into the present through characters’ individual memories and through collectively remembered history. Places remember past events and become attached to mental states in the present, making even the city a text to read. Distinctive details and phrases recur repeatedly, establishing connections across dozens or even hundreds of pages and requiring readers to step outside the temporal sequence, viewing events from an Olympian perspective. Characters’ voices compete with third-person narration for control of the story, and new kinds of narration emerge from chapter to chapter, changing the rules of the game whenever one begins to feel too comfortable.

Challenges like these impel readers to look for help outside the text, and over the course of nearly a century commentators have labored to supply it, in the process becoming better readers themselves. But even with help from people who know the book well and can communicate its mysteries effectively, there is only so much that anyone can take in on a first reading. Until the shape of the entire book is imprinted in one’s memory, like the map of an often-walked city, too much information can only be an annoyance hindering one’s passage from sentence to sentence. After that threshold is crossed, more begins to be better.

The online notes that I have been publishing for about ten years address such challenges, not least the gulf separating new and experienced readers. First-time readers of the novel want just enough commentary to make some minimal sense of a baffling sentence, freeing them to move on to the next one. Veterans want anything but that. The solution I have devised is to begin each note with an introductory paragraph that conveys the most essential contextual information and some succinct suggestion of how to use it, and then to build “in the broadest way immarginable,” as Joyce said in Finnegans Wake. Readers who click on a “Read more” button after the first paragraph can explore an allusion in more detail, think about how knowing it may shape the reading process, entertain alternative interpretations, attend to what various scholars have written, make connections to other passages in the novel, and so on. These later paragraphs speak primarily to more experienced readers. The opening paragraphs, and this Preface, are addressed primarily to newcomers.

Sahas Subramanian created the ebook that you are viewing in order to provide a downloadable copy of my annotated Ulysses that users can store on their devices and consult even when not connected to the internet. As I keep adding notes to the website, and sometimes revising, his software will make weekly updates. Before you begin reading, it may be helpful to learn a few things about the ebook, the website, and annotation generally.

  • Annotators of Ulysses must strike a balance between informing and interpreting. Neither exists in a pure state: simply noting an allusion to something outside the text constitutes an act of interpretation, and interpretations uninformed by such contexts will gain little traction. Still, commentators tend to emphasize one or the other. Some try to be as objective as possible, briefly detailing things like literary references, historical events, song lyrics, and Dublin locations, while not telling readers how to use the information. Others act as tour guides, isolating interesting and important features of each chapter and suggesting paths for a reader to take through it. My notes begin with information, identifying problematic issues and offering contexts for understanding them, but they also explore the consequences that follow from accepting a context as relevant. The goal is not to perform readings of the entire novel, but to better appreciate significant threads running through Joyce’s incredibly elaborate tapestry.

  • On the principle that conceptual learning is facilitated by visual and auditory learning, all the commentary on the website is accompanied by still images and/or sound recordings and videos. Some of these multimedia frames in the note sidebars use photographs, artworks, and graphics simply to illustrate things discussed in the note. Others purvey more directly useful resources like annotated Dublin maps, photographs and recordings from Joyce’s era, old advertisements, physical objects, newspaper columns, musical performances, streets, buildings, and landforms. The site also has a comprehensive alphabetical list of “People in the Novel” (there are hundreds) and a not yet comprehensive chronological list of “Times in the Novel,” beginning long before June 16, 1904. Ebook readers will miss these resources unless they make a practice of checking the website from time to time.

  • The website uses hyperlinks within the text rather than note numbers, in six colors that signal different kinds of content addressed within the notes. This allows readers to concentrate on topics that interest them as they travel through the novel. Rather than hyperlinks the ebook employs traditional note numbers, but Subramanian has replicated the colors of my links in his note numbers for anyone who wishes to read notes on the land, culture, and history of Ireland (green), various kinds of Literature (orange), the cityscape of Dublin (brown), musical and dramatic Performances (purple), issues pertaining to the human Body (red), or the artistic choices of the Writer (blue). Sometimes a colored symbol within a note will signal a transition to a new kind of content. A more detailed listing of the topics addressed in my six categories can be found on the website under “Color Coding.” An ebook without the colors is also available.

  • Many notes on the website, probably a majority of them, are linked to more than one passage in the novel. This practice allows me to join closely related passages, both within chapters and in different chapters, and to write essay-like notes that do not simply explicate local cruxes but address larger issues within the novel. Different note numbers within the ebook may sometimes take you to the same note, and in notes glossing multiple passages you may find that your question about a passage is not well answered in the opening paragraph. These would be times to “Read more,” moving on to the paragraph that addresses your question. To help readers find what they are looking for in longer notes, and to save them from having to toggle back and forth between novel and commentary, I quote generously from the passages being glossed and emphasize the most relevant parts with boldfaced type. Looking for a boldfaced quotation of your passage can help you quickly narrow your search.

  • For anyone who wishes to consult these notes in conjunction with a traditional book, the website offers a way to divide its continuous electronic text to match the pagination of five commonly sold print editions. Lineation is not reproduced, only page numbers, but this feature of the site can be very useful for locating passages within a printed text. There are several reasons why you might want to do this, including a wish to record your own notes within the margins of a printed text while using the ebook or the website.

  • I am the sole author of more than 98% of the notes on the website. Other people have written the remaining ones, or co-written them with me. Authors and years of electronic publication are recorded at the bottom of notes, but this information is not reproduced in the ebook. Many notes cite the findings of other scholars, whether from print sources, websites, or personal communications, with fuller information supplied on “Sources” and “Contributors” pages on the website. These pages too are absent from the ebook.

  • In keeping with the cross-referential, recursive, and cumulative nature of the novel, most of the website’s notes contain links to other notes. As with the infinitely extendable notes, the idea is to make more information available at every step. One result may be that, rather than simply taking up topics as they arise sequentially in the course of reading, a user will follow other paths through Joyce’s maze, much as looking up one word in a dictionary may lead to others, or (a more exact analogy) investigating one topic on Wikipedia may lead to other topics through the hyperlinks in the article. The ebook does not reproduce these links—which may not be a bad thing for people trying to get through the book for the first time!

  • The ebook text, which is being automatically updated to mirror the text on the website, is not precisely identical to the Gutenberg version of the novel or to that of any print edition. It represents my own ongoing effort to approximate an ideal text, choosing among literally thousands of textual variations from edition to edition of Ulysses—most of them small, but some quite consequential. Some notes discuss the editorial issues involved in these choices, usually long after the “Read more” division.

  • Stopping at “Read more” will spare you the curse of being overwhelmed by too much secondary material, but it may also leave you unsatisfied. You will need to work out a balance between reading too little and too much—a quandary that is not unique to this collection of annotations, but bedevils all first-time readers of Ulysses. Avoid published commentary altogether and most of the book flies right over your head. Dig into it too deeply and you lose the absorbing, enjoyable flow of reading a novel. Many coping strategies have been suggested, such as reading a paragraph or page without notes, then reading the relevant notes, and then reading the text a second time. You will need to find your own best practice by trial and error.

  • Since my guiding principle is to give users as much as they can handle, the notes may contain “spoilers.” Some annotators believe that buried surprises should be left for neophytes to discover on their own, much later. I resist this view, for several reasons: Ulysses is not the kind of book that can be ruined by knowing what happens; first-timers need all the help they can get; and withholding assistance perpetuates the privilege of those in the know, essentially taunting others to prove their worth. In my opinion life is too short, and the book too difficult, for such an exclusionary attitude. Nevertheless, when some disclosure could spoil the pleasurable challenge of individual discovery I try to keep it out of the opening paragraph. People intrepid enough to read more will be admitted to the insiders’ club free of charge, except perhaps for loss of innocence.

Finally, a few words about my own involvement. The Joyce Project started as an idea for helping my students. I envisioned a site where all their questions could be addressed by clicking on links rather than having to flip through multiple reference works. But as I started writing notes I saw that I would not be content with simply reproducing other people’s observations, or with presenting issues in a cursory way. Tracking down information on my own meant weighing evidence more actively than when I was merely trying to summarize others’ findings. Addressing multiple passages within notes meant asking questions that had not always been asked before. Scrutinizing possible sources and contexts led me to discoveries not recorded in other commentaries. And, most crucially, trying to explain exactly how some background information might shape a reading of a passage meant that I was forever seeing new things in Joyce’s text. Instead of mere crutches for a beginner limping through the book, my notes were becoming platforms on which textual problems could be laid out in detail and examined from many angles. I had to write notes in my own way, at greater length, and devise a new form for doing it. The project became much bigger than I had imagined.

One published commentator has remarked that the joys of Ulysses belong to the reader and not to the annotator. Not so for me! Each new note now feels to me like a portal of discovery, to borrow a phrase from the novel’s ninth chapter. When I first read this book as an ignorant but eager college student, I knew that it was extraordinary and would draw me back. More than four decades later it draws me back still, with no less freshness than the first time, and with no less sense that I have much to learn. Looking carefully at some long-familiar passage I find, again and again, that I have not remembered something quite exactly enough, or looked quite hard enough for analogues and implications. Some little detail of Joyce’s choosing makes the book even more astonishingly hyper-significant than I thought. This sense of unextinguished discovery drives me to keep writing notes as I begin my second year of retirement, and I hope it will do so for years to come. For anyone trying to read the daunting masterpiece for the first time, I hope my notes will give you the threads you need to find your own way through the labyrinth, and I hope that you too will find a life’s companion in this remarkable work.



John Hunt
Missoula, Montana
